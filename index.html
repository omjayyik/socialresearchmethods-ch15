Writing the Report & Navigating the Politics of Research

Part 1: The Research Report

A research project is not complete until the findings are shared. The research report is the primary vehicle for communicating how a study was conducted and what was discovered. Good writing is not a luxury; it is essential for clear communication.

"We don't want imperfection in our prose to interfere with our readers' understanding." - Howard Becker

The Quantitative Report: A Standardized Structure

Follows a logical, linear path from hypothesis to conclusion.

  1. Abstract: A brief summary of the entire study.
  2. Introduction: States the research problem and its significance.
  3. Literature Review: Situates the study within the existing body of research.
  4. Methodology: Details exactly how the study was conducted (e.g., sample, measures, procedures). This section should be a "recipe" that another researcher could follow to replicate the study.
  5. Results: Presents the descriptive and inferential statistics in tables, charts, and graphs. This is a straightforward presentation of the facts, with minimal interpretation.
  6. Discussion: Interprets the results. What do the numbers mean? How do they relate back to the hypotheses and the literature?
  7. Conclusion: Summarizes the key findings and may suggest directions for future research.

The Qualitative Report: A Narrative Approach

More flexible and often longer, weaving data and analysis together into a compelling story.

  • Less Formulaic: Does not follow a rigid section-by-section format. The structure is often organized around key themes or a chronological narrative.
  • Integration of Data & Analysis: Evidence (e.g., quotes, detailed descriptions) is presented alongside the researcher's interpretation. The goal is to avoid the "error of segregation" where data and analysis are too far apart.
  • Emphasis on Voice & Reflexivity: The researcher's own perspective and role in the research process are often acknowledged.
  • Common Genres: Can take different forms, such as a "Realist Tale" (objective third-person account) or a "Confessional Tale" (first-person account focusing on the researcher's experience).

Part 2: The Politics of Social Research

Research does not happen in a vacuum. It is a social and political activity, subject to external pressures and influences, especially when it touches on sensitive or controversial topics.

Funding & Sponsorship

Who pays for the research can influence what gets studied and, sometimes, what gets found. Researchers must be transparent about their funding sources.

Access to Data

Gaining access to participants or data often requires navigating powerful gatekeepers who may have their own agendas or wish to suppress certain findings.

Dissemination & Use of Findings

Once published, research findings enter the public domain where they can be used, misused, or distorted by politicians, media, and special interest groups to support their own positions.

Objectivity & Value Freedom

Can‚Äîand should‚Äîsocial research be truly objective and free from the researcher's personal values? This is one of the most enduring debates in the social sciences.

Positivist View

Strives for a "value-free" science. Researchers should be neutral and objective, keeping their personal values separate from their scientific work.

Interpretive View

Believes complete value-freedom is impossible. The best a researcher can do is be aware of their own values ("value-relative") and be open and honest about them.

Critical View

Rejects the idea of value-free research as a myth. Argues that all research is "value-laden" and that researchers should be morally committed to using their work to challenge injustice and empower the oppressed.
